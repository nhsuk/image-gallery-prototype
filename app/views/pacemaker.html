Overview - Pacemaker implantation

A pacemaker is a small device that sends electrical signals to your heart to help it beat regularly.

Fitting a pacemaker (called implantation) is a very common type of heart surgery.

A pacemaker can greatly improve your quality of life if you have problems with your heart, like slow or irregular heartbeats. It can also be lifesaving if you have a high chance of a cardiac arrest or some types of heart disease.

What pacemaker are used for

Your heart is a muscle thatâ€™s controlled by electrical signals. Sometimes these signals are not received properly, which means the heart cannot beat normally.

Heart conditions that may be helped by a pacemaker include:

  • a heart that beats too slowly (bradycardia)
  • a heart that beats too fast (tachycardia)
  • irregular heartbeats (a heart block)
  • cardiac arrest, which is when the heart stops beating completely

How a pacemaker is implanted

The operation to implant a pacemaker is done by a heart surgeon.

It's usually done using local anaesthetic, which means you'll be awake during the procedure but will not feel any pain.

The pacemaker is usually placed under your skin near your collarbone, on the left side of your chest. The wires that are attached to the device are then guided through a blood vessel to your heart.

You will probably be in the operating theatre for around 1 hour, and most people are able to leave hospital on the same day, or on the following day.
